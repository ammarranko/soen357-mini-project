01

Introduction

Context

How many times have you forgotten to take your medication? Patients with chronic conditions frequently struggle to manage complex medication schedules and keep track of upcoming doctor's appointments. Existing digital solutions are often either too complex or too fragmented to be truly helpful.

The goal of this project was to design MedicNova, a health companion "super app" that consolidates reminders, medication tracking, appointment scheduling, and direct communication with healthcare professionals into one seamless, stress-free experience.

Problem Statement

The main challenges from a user perspective break down as follows:

  • Remember medications consistently: users often forget doses due to busy schedules, interruptions, or inconsistent routines.
  • Track adherence over time: users need a simple way to log doses and review patterns.
  • Reduce "Did I take it?" uncertainty: users frequently second-guess themselves and need clear confirmation.
  • Coordinate appointments: users struggle to keep up with follow-ups, renewals, and medical visits.
  • Communicate easily with healthcare professionals: users want to share accurate medication info for better care decisions.

Design Goals

Since illness does not discriminate by age, MedicNova is designed to be adaptable for a wide range of users. The core focus is simplicity: clear, direct, and free of unnecessary clutter.

  • Increase adherence through reliable, actionable reminders
  • Reduce uncertainty through visible confirmation states (Taken + timestamp)
  • Support busy contexts via minimal-step logging
  • Avoid notification fatigue via flexible reminder styles
  • Support accessibility with readable UI and large tap targets

Target Users & Scope

Primary users are adults who manage their own medications (typically 2-5 doses per day). Secondary users are caregivers who manage medications on behalf of a family member or dependent. The prototype scope covers the core medication management workflow (reminders + tracking), appointment scheduling, and messaging with healthcare professionals.

02

Research

To build a medication app that truly works, I needed to understand real-life habits. I started with a user survey, then backed up those findings with a literature review of healthcare and HCI research, combining direct feedback with established science.

Primary Research - Survey

A survey of 12 participants gathered data on medication loads, missed-dose frequency, tools used, frustrations, and feature priorities. Here are the key takeaways:

83% self-manage medication
75% miss doses weekly or more
92% rated reliable reminders 5/5
58% use phone alarms as strategy

Top reasons for missing doses: too busy (33%), forgot (33%), unsure if already taken (25%). Top usability frustrations with existing apps: small text (33%), too many steps (25%).

→ Access full survey results

Secondary Research - Literature

The WHO reports that adherence to long-term therapies averages approximately 50% in developed countries. Mobile-based reminder interventions increase compliance, especially when users can actively confirm actions rather than relying on passive alerts. From an HCI perspective, reducing cognitive load is essential: visible confirmation of completed actions reduces uncertainty and error. Research on notifications shows that excessive alerts contribute to disengagement; adaptive strategies improve long-term engagement.

Key Findings

Design Decisions from Research

1

Interactive Reminders

One-tap Taken / Snooze / Skip with immediate state feedback, because missed doses are frequent even with alarms.

2

Highly Visible Status

Show last-taken time, today's medication list, and next scheduled dose prominently to eliminate "did I take it?" uncertainty.

3

Minimal Steps with Confirmation

Marking a dose takes two taps or fewer. A two-tap confirmation creates a mindful moment, cementing the action in memory while preventing accidental misclicks.

4

Adaptive Notifications

Users choose Discreet / Persistent / Adaptive. If no response, escalate gradually. Max reminders configurable to avoid spamming.

5

Scalable "Today" View

Medications grouped by time blocks (Morning / Afternoon / Evening) with clear status and easy access to details.

6

Clear Navigation

Bottom nav with icons for core features. Settings moved to top-right to maximize space for daily essentials.

Personas

Three personas were developed from the survey and literature, each representing a distinct usage focus within MedicNova, together covering all core workflows.

Busy Self-Manager

Sarah Valkov

Sarah represents the most common survey pattern: self-managed medication in a busy lifestyle, where doses are missed due to being busy or forgetting. She needs fast, low-friction interactions: one-tap actions and minimal steps.

Clarity & Accessibility Seeker

David Rando

David represents low-tech users who struggle with verification and confidence, especially with multiple medications. He highlights why MedicNova must prioritize visible confirmation states, timestamps, and accessible UI defaults.

Care Coordinator

Leila

Leila relies on healthcare tools for care coordination: appointment scheduling and doctor messaging. She ensures the design supports the broader workflow of managing health beyond daily reminders.

07

Sketching, Wireframing & Prototype

Initial Sketches

Before conducting the survey, several ideas were explored: some kept, others discarded once survey results showed they weren't aligned with user needs. For example, early sketches featured a constant loud reminder with no limit. Research revealed this could cause notification fatigue, leading to a more adaptive system with Discreet, Persistent, or Adaptive styles.

Wireframes

These wireframes translate the research into structured layouts: focusing on information hierarchy, interaction patterns, and navigation logic. They include a mix of large-text accessibility views and standard views.

Prototype

After exploring ideas through sketches and refining them into wireframes, the prototype was built by applying colors and detailed UI elements, bringing the concept to life as a clickable, interactive, demo-ready experience.

08

Usability Testing Plan

The purpose of usability testing is to evaluate whether users can complete MedicNova's core workflows efficiently and confidently, identifying issues in navigation clarity, interaction speed, and confirmation states.

Method & Tools

Remote, unmoderated usability testing via Maze, collecting task success/failure, time on task, misclicks, path analysis, and embedded post-task questions. A sample of at least 20 participants who manage their own medications daily.

Evaluation criteria:

  • Effectiveness: can users complete tasks correctly (success rate, errors)?
  • Efficiency: how quickly and in how many steps do they complete tasks?
  • Satisfaction: how confident do users feel (qualitative feedback)?

Test Tasks

1

Reminder → Log Taken

"You receive a reminder. Confirm you took the medication."

2

Verify Confirmation

"Verify the dose was logged and check the time it was taken."

3

Schedule Appointment

"Schedule a doctor appointment for next week and add a short note."

4

Message Doctor

"Send a message to the doctor/clinic and attach a medication log."

Analysis & Iteration Strategy

Issues will be grouped by severity: High (blocks task completion), Medium (causes friction), and Low (minor visual adjustments). Changes may include improving Taken status visibility, reducing steps in the reminder workflow, and strengthening confirmation feedback.

09

Reflection

"This process reinforced the value of evidence-based iteration: designing with users in mind, validating assumptions through research, and preparing usability testing to guide improvements."

Following the UX design process helped me move from a broad idea, "a health companion app," to a solution grounded in specific user needs. By combining primary survey research with secondary literature, I confirmed that medication non-adherence is linked to forgetfulness, routine disruption, and cognitive overload rather than lack of motivation.

Before research, I assumed stronger reminders automatically lead to better adherence. Early sketches featured constant, loud reminders with no limit. Survey feedback made it clear this could backfire: frequent interruptions overwhelm users. I replaced the no-limit approach with selectable reminder styles (Discreet, Persistent, Adaptive) and a Snooze option. Good design doesn't push users harder; it supports them in realistic contexts.

Creating personas, journey maps, and a storyboard made the problem more concrete by revealing where breakdowns happen in everyday life. These insights directly influenced the wireframes and prototype: making reminder interactions immediately actionable, ensuring "Taken" status and timestamps are easy to find, and keeping key actions accessible from the Home screen.

The main challenge was scope control. It was easy to overdesign beyond the core problem. I addressed this by prioritizing workflows most strongly supported by research and integrating appointments and messaging as focused supporting flows rather than competing features.
